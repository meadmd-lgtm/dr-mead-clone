## Normal Bowel Function

The minimum healthy bowel movement frequency is once per day. This is a bare minimum, not a goal. Ideally, a person should have two to three well-formed, easy-to-pass bowel movements per day — one after each major meal is normal and healthy.

The conventional medical definition of "normal" includes movements as infrequent as three per week. This is not health. It is accepted dysfunction. A person having three bowel movements a week is keeping waste and toxins in their body for days longer than they should be. Those toxins are being reabsorbed.

Stool quality matters as much as frequency. Healthy stool should be soft, well-formed, medium brown, easy to pass without straining, and should not float (floating stool often indicates fat malabsorption or excess gas).

## The Transit Time Test

Healthy transit time — the time it takes for food to travel from mouth to elimination — is 12 to 24 hours. Most Americans have transit times of 40 to 70 hours or longer. Some people I have tested have transit times over 100 hours, which means food consumed on Monday is not leaving the body until Thursday.

Test protocol: Eat a significant amount of beets (for red color) or take activated charcoal capsules (for black color). Note the exact time. Watch for the color change in stool. The time between consumption and the first appearance of the color indicator is your transit time.

12 to 18 hours: excellent
18 to 24 hours: good
24 to 48 hours: slow, intervention needed
Over 48 hours: significantly impaired, priority for correction

## Diverticula and Bowel Pockets

Diverticula are small pockets or pouches that form in the walls of the colon due to chronic pressure from straining and constipation. Once formed, these pockets can trap waste material that does not move with normal peristalsis.

This trapped material becomes a reservoir for bacterial overgrowth and toxin accumulation. When diverticula become inflamed, this is called diverticulitis — a painful and potentially serious condition.

The medical approach is to treat acute flares with antibiotics and surgery in severe cases. My approach is to restore bowel motility, soften stool, and use herbal antimicrobials and probiotics to maintain a healthy microbial environment in these pockets.

Diverticula also harbor parasites. In my clinical experience, parasite burden is far more common than mainstream medicine acknowledges, and the colon — particularly diverticula — is a primary residence. A thorough colon cleanse protocol, including anti-parasitic herbs, is essential for anyone with diverticular disease.

## Colon Cleanse Protocol

My colon cleanse protocol varies by patient but typically includes the following elements:

Hydration: The most basic requirement for healthy elimination is adequate water. Most constipated patients are chronically dehydrated. I recommend half the body weight in ounces of filtered water daily, with electrolytes (trace minerals or pinch of sea salt).

Fiber increase: Soluble fiber from fruits, vegetables, and psyllium husk feeds beneficial bacteria and adds bulk to stool. Insoluble fiber from vegetables speeds transit. I avoid fiber supplements that are loaded with additives.

Magnesium: Magnesium draws water into the colon and relaxes the smooth muscle of the intestinal wall. Magnesium citrate or oxide (both have a laxative effect) at 300 to 600 mg at night is one of the most effective natural tools for constipation.

Triphala: An Ayurvedic formula of three fruits (amalaki, bibhitaki, haritaki) that is a gentle but effective colon tonic. It tones the colon muscle, promotes peristalsis, and has antimicrobial properties. I consider this one of the safest long-term colon support formulas.

Cascara Sagrada and Senna: Stronger herbal laxatives for acute constipation. Used short-term to get things moving. Not for long-term daily use as the colon can become dependent.

Colon hydrotherapy (colonics): A series of professional colon irrigation sessions is one of the most effective tools for deep colon cleansing. A good colon hydrotherapist can feel areas of the colon that are impacted or in spasm and work with them. I recommend a series of three to six sessions for patients with a significant history of constipation.

Probiotics: Reseeding the colon with healthy bacteria is essential after any cleanse. I recommend high-dose, multi-strain probiotics with both Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species, taken at night away from food.

Anti-parasitic herbs: Black walnut hull, wormwood, and cloves are the classic anti-parasitic triad. I add this component for any patient with a history of significant constipation, diverticular disease, or international travel.

# Vitamin D Protocol — Dr. Mead

## Why Vitamin D Is Not Really a Vitamin

Vitamin D is misnamed. It is actually a steroid hormone that regulates over 2,000 genes in the human body. It controls immune function, inflammation, calcium metabolism, insulin sensitivity, mood, cancer prevention, cardiovascular health, and much more. Calling it a vitamin severely underestimates its importance.

Vitamin D deficiency is one of the most widespread undiagnosed conditions I see. In my clinical experience, the majority of patients who come to me with chronic illness, weight problems, immune dysfunction, or depression are significantly deficient in vitamin D. Yet it is rarely tested comprehensively and even more rarely supplemented to therapeutic levels.

## The Conversion Pathway

Understanding how vitamin D is made and activated explains why so many people are deficient even in sunny climates.

Step 1: UVB rays from the sun strike the skin and convert 7-dehydrocholesterol (a cholesterol derivative) into vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol). This only happens with UVB rays, which are blocked by glass, sunscreen, and are absent at high latitudes in winter months and during early morning and late afternoon hours.

Step 2: D3 travels to the liver, where it is converted to 25-hydroxyvitamin D (calcidiol). This is what a standard blood test measures.

Step 3: The kidneys (and other tissues) convert calcidiol into 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol), which is the biologically active hormone form.

This multi-step conversion means that problems at any stage — poor sun exposure, liver congestion, kidney dysfunction, magnesium deficiency — result in deficiency even with adequate dietary intake or supplementation.

Magnesium is critically important: it is required at multiple steps in the vitamin D conversion pathway. A patient who is magnesium deficient cannot activate vitamin D properly, regardless of how much they supplement. I always address magnesium alongside vitamin D.

## Testing and Optimal Ranges

The test to order is 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25-OH vitamin D). The lab reference range is typically 30 to 100 ng/mL, but this is far too wide to be clinically useful.

Dr. Mead's optimal range: 60 to 80 ng/mL for general health and immune function. Many researchers suggest 80 to 100 ng/mL for cancer prevention and serious immune conditions.

Below 30 ng/mL: deficient — clinical intervention required
30 to 50 ng/mL: insufficient — suboptimal function
50 to 60 ng/mL: low normal — improvement needed
60 to 80 ng/mL: optimal
Above 100 ng/mL: potentially excessive — monitor with cofactors

## The Sunscreen Problem

Sunscreen is one of the primary drivers of the modern vitamin D deficiency epidemic. SPF 30 blocks approximately 97% of UVB rays. SPF 50 blocks approximately 98%. This means that a person who religiously applies sunscreen before going outside is receiving almost no vitamin D synthesis from sun exposure.

We have been told for decades to avoid the sun and use high SPF sunscreen at all times. The result is a population that is profoundly deficient in the single most important immune-regulating hormone the body produces.

My position: sensible sun exposure without sunscreen — enough to stimulate D3 synthesis without burning — is one of the most health-promoting things a person can do. Start with 10 to 20 minutes of midday sun exposure with significant skin area exposed. Build up gradually. Avoid burning. After achieving a therapeutic sun tan, the risk of burning decreases significantly as melanin provides natural protection.

When sunscreen is used (extended outdoor exposure, preventing burn), choose mineral sunscreens with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide rather than chemical sunscreens, which are themselves endocrine disruptors.

## The UV Bulb Solution

For patients in northern latitudes, those who work indoors, or those who cannot get adequate sun exposure, I recommend a narrowband UVB lamp (311 nanometer). This replicates the specific UVB wavelength that stimulates D3 synthesis in the skin.

These lamps are medically used for psoriasis treatment and are available for home use. Protocol: expose significant skin area (arms, legs, torso) for the recommended time per the lamp's instructions. Start with very short sessions (1 to 2 minutes) and gradually increase. Never look directly at the lamp.

This is preferable to oral supplementation alone because it produces D3 in the skin exactly as nature intended, and the body regulates the conversion so that toxicity from excess production is essentially impossible, unlike with oral megadoses.

## Supplementation Guidelines

When sun exposure is inadequate, oral vitamin D3 supplementation is warranted. Important considerations:

Always supplement D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2 (ergocalciferol). D2 is less bioavailable and does not raise serum levels as effectively.

Always take vitamin K2 alongside vitamin D3. Vitamin D increases calcium absorption, and K2 directs that calcium into bones and teeth rather than into soft tissue and arteries. MK-7 is the preferred form of K2. Without K2, high-dose vitamin D can contribute to arterial calcification.

Take vitamin D3 with the largest meal of the day, as it is fat-soluble and requires dietary fat for absorption.

Typical therapeutic doses: 5,000 to 10,000 IU daily, depending on baseline levels and individual response. I recommend testing after 3 months of supplementation to assess whether levels have reached the optimal range.

Magnesium supplementation (300 to 400 mg magnesium glycinate or malate at night) is essential alongside vitamin D.

## Vitamin D Deficiency Symptoms

Fatigue and low energy even with adequate sleep
Frequent illness or slow recovery from infections
Depression, low mood, seasonal affective disorder
Bone pain or muscle weakness
Hair loss
Poor wound healing
Chronic pain or inflammation
Brain fog and difficulty concentrating
Insulin resistance and difficulty losing weight
Autoimmune flares

Many of these symptoms overlap directly with thyroid dysfunction and adrenal fatigue, which is why I always test vitamin D as part of a comprehensive evaluation.

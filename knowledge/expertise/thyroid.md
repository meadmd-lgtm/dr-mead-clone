# Thyroid Protocol — Dr. Mead

## My Clinical Philosophy on Thyroid

Most people with thyroid problems have been told their labs are "normal." That is because conventional medicine only tests TSH, which is a pituitary hormone — not even a thyroid hormone. It tells you what the brain is asking for, not what the thyroid is actually delivering. I have seen hundreds of patients with textbook hypothyroid symptoms whose TSH was completely normal. When we ran a full panel, the problem was obvious.

The thyroid is the master metabolic regulator. When it is not working, the body cannot burn fat efficiently, maintain body temperature, sustain energy, or clear toxins. Weight loss becomes nearly impossible when the thyroid is compromised.

## The Full Thyroid Panel I Run

I never diagnose from TSH alone. A complete evaluation includes:

TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) — this is the pituitary signal. High TSH means the brain is screaming at the thyroid to work harder. But optimal is 1.0 to 2.0, not the lab's "normal" range of 0.5 to 4.5.

Free T4 — the storage hormone made by the thyroid. The raw material. Optimal is in the upper third of the reference range.

Free T3 — the active hormone that enters cells and drives metabolism. This is what actually burns fat. Many people convert T4 to T3 poorly due to gut inflammation, stress, or nutrient deficiencies. Optimal Free T3 is in the upper third of range.

Reverse T3 — the brake. Under chronic stress, the body converts T4 into Reverse T3 instead of Free T3, shutting down metabolism as a survival response. A high Reverse T3 to Free T3 ratio is a classic finding in people who cannot lose weight despite doing everything right.

TPO Antibodies (Thyroid Peroxidase) — these indicate Hashimoto's disease, an autoimmune attack on the thyroid. Conventional medicine often ignores antibodies if TSH is normal, but antibodies cause progressive thyroid destruction and need to be addressed.

TgAb (Thyroglobulin Antibodies) — a second autoimmune marker. Both TPO and TgAb should be under 4.

## The Basal Temperature Test

Lab tests tell part of the story. Body temperature tells the rest. The thyroid regulates metabolic heat. A consistently low basal temperature is one of the most reliable signs of hypothyroid function, even when labs look normal.

Protocol: Place a thermometer on the nightstand before bed. First thing in the morning, before getting up or moving, take your axillary (armpit) temperature. Hold the thermometer for a full 10 minutes. Record for 5 days. Women should do this on days 2 through 5 of the menstrual cycle.

Optimal basal temperature: 97.8 to 98.2 degrees Fahrenheit.
Below 97.6: significant hypothyroid indication.
Below 97.0: strong clinical hypothyroid pattern regardless of labs.

## The Halogen Problem

The thyroid requires iodine to make thyroid hormones. The problem is that chlorine, fluoride, and bromine are all halogens — the same chemical family as iodine. They compete for the same receptors in the thyroid and in thyroid hormone production. When these toxic halogens are high in the body, iodine cannot do its job.

Sources of toxic halogen exposure:
- Fluoride: fluoridated municipal water, toothpaste, non-stick cookware coatings
- Chlorine: municipal water (showering and bathing exposes more chlorine than drinking), bleach, pools
- Bromine: brominated vegetable oils in soft drinks, commercial bread (potassium bromate), some medications, hot tub chemicals

Addressing halogen toxicity is a prerequisite to restoring thyroid function. I recommend filtered water (remove chlorine and fluoride), a whole house filter for showering, removing fluoride toothpaste, and ensuring adequate iodine and selenium intake to support the displacement of these halogens.

Key nutrients that support thyroid function:
- Iodine and tyrosine (raw materials for T4 synthesis)
- Selenium (converts T4 to T3 and protects the thyroid from oxidative damage)
- Zinc (required for thyroid hormone receptor sensitivity)
- Vitamin D (immune regulation, critical for Hashimoto's)
- Magnesium (involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions including thyroid conversion)

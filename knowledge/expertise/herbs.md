## My Relationship with Plant Medicine

I have been working with herbs my entire professional life. My training in indigenous medicine deepened my relationship with plants from a clinical tool to something more fundamental — an understanding that human beings co-evolved with the plant kingdom and that plants carry intelligence our bodies recognize.

Modern pharmaceutical medicine isolates single active compounds and amplifies them to therapeutic doses. Herbal medicine works differently. Plants contain hundreds of compounds that work synergistically, often with buffers and modulators built in that reduce side effects and improve bioavailability. This is why herbs often have broader safety profiles than isolated pharmaceutical compounds.

## Wildcrafting — Harvesting from Nature

Wildcrafting is the practice of harvesting plants from their natural environment. A wildcrafted herb gathered at the right time, from clean land, in its natural ecosystem, is categorically different from a greenhouse-cultivated supplement.

Basic wildcrafting principles:

Ethical harvesting: Never take more than one-third of any stand. Leave enough for the plant to regenerate and for wildlife that depends on it. Harvest with gratitude and intention.

Correct identification: Know the plant completely before harvesting. Use multiple identification features — leaf shape, arrangement, stem texture, smell, habitat, season. Mistakes in identification can be dangerous.

Timing: Roots are best harvested in fall when the plant's energy has returned to the root. Leaves are best harvested just before or during flowering when they are at peak medicinal potency. Flowers are harvested at full bloom. Bark is harvested in spring when sap is rising.

Clean land: Harvest away from roadsides, agricultural fields (pesticides), and areas with potential heavy metal contamination. Know your land.

## Herbal Preparations

Different preparations extract different constituents and have different applications.

Tincture: A concentrated alcohol extract. The plant material is macerated (soaked) in a mixture of food-grade alcohol and water — typically 60 to 70 percent alcohol for most plants. The alcohol extracts both water-soluble and fat-soluble compounds. Standard maceration time is four to six weeks with periodic shaking. The mixture is then pressed and strained. Dosage is typically one to two dropperfuls (30 to 60 drops) in water, two to three times daily. Tinctures have a long shelf life (three to five years or more) and are highly bioavailable.

Standard infusion (tea): Boiling water poured over dried or fresh plant material, steeped for 10 to 20 minutes, and strained. Best for leaves and flowers. The medicinal standard is one heaping tablespoon of dried herb per cup of water. Teas are gentle and appropriate for long-term daily use.

Decoction: Used for roots, bark, seeds, and tough plant material that requires more heat to extract. The plant material is simmered (not boiled hard) in water for 20 to 45 minutes, then strained. The longer simmering breaks down the cellular structure and releases compounds that a simple infusion would not extract.

Poultice: Fresh or rehydrated dried plant material applied directly to the skin. The plant is mashed, bruised, or chewed to release its compounds, then applied to a wound, inflammation, or affected area and held in place with a cloth. Plantain poultice for insect stings, comfrey poultice for bruises and bone injuries, and cabbage leaf poultice for mastitis and joint inflammation are classic applications.

Salve: An herbal-infused oil combined with beeswax to create a semi-solid topical preparation. First, plant material is infused in a carrier oil (olive oil, coconut oil, jojoba) — either by cold infusion over 4 to 6 weeks or by gentle heat infusion over several hours. The infused oil is then combined with melted beeswax (typically 1 ounce of beeswax per cup of oil) and poured into tins or jars to cool and solidify. Salves are excellent for skin healing, inflammation, pain relief, and wound care.

Fomentation: A cloth soaked in a strong herbal tea or decoction and applied warm to the body. Used for muscle pain, lymph congestion, and sinus issues.

Syrup: A decoction or infusion combined with honey or vegetable glycerine as a preservative and to improve palatability. Elderberry syrup for immune support is the classic example. Excellent for children and for respiratory herbs.

## Indigenous Medicine Perspective

My doctorate in indigenous medicine has given me a perspective on plant medicine that I cannot get from Western herbalism texts alone. Indigenous healing traditions understand that plant medicine is not simply chemistry. Plants are relationships. The healer's relationship with the plant, the patient's intention to heal, and the plant's own intelligence all participate in the healing.

Indigenous traditions often prescribe whole plant preparations rather than standardized extracts, understanding that the full spectrum of compounds works together. They use ceremony and intention alongside medicine, recognizing that healing operates on physical, emotional, and spiritual levels simultaneously.

I integrate this understanding into my practice. A patient who believes they can heal, who is engaged with their own healing process, and who approaches their medicine with respect and attention gets better results than one who passively takes supplements while remaining disconnected from the process.

This is not mysticism — it is an acknowledgment that healing is whole-person work.
